Pentatonics

Five notes, endless rooms.

The vocabulary that pours through every great solo — and the rules for where each one lives.

Major Pentatonic

1 · 2 · 3 · 5 · 6

The default major-key voice. Bright, open, unfussy.

  • I∆7, ii7, vi7, VI∆7
  • bVII7#11 (lydian dominant)
  • On b5 of dom7alt — Gb maj pent over C7alt

Minor Pentatonic

1 · b3 · 4 · 5 · b7

The workhorse. Every chord type has one waiting for it.

  • Over dom7: minor pent up a whole step (Am over G7)
  • Over m7: on the 1, 2, and 5 (Dm, Em, Am over Dm7)
  • Over ∆7: down a 3rd, plus 5th, plus 5th-above

Dorian Pentatonic

1 · b3 · 4 · 5 · 6

Minor pentatonic with the 6th instead of the b7. The Dorian color, distilled.

  • Over m7 — pure Dorian without the modal commitment of the full scale
  • Over dom7 — on the 5th degree (D Dorian pent over G7)

Dominant Pentatonic

1 · 2 · 3 · 5 · b7

Mixolydian, distilled. McCoy's primary color.

  • Native voice over dom7
  • Stack on the root and a half-step up (C & Db over a C pedal)
  • Cycle: 1-3-b7-2-5 → 2-5-1-3-b7 →…

Altered Pentatonic

1 · b3 · 3 · b5 · b7

Tension scale for V7alt resolutions.

  • On b5 of dom7alt — Db alt pent over G7alt
  • Pairs with hexatonic altered for thicker color

m6 (Coltrane) Pentatonic

1 · 2 · b3 · 5 · 6

A single shape that unlocks an entire Coltrane progression.

  • In root position: i6, VIIalt, IV9, vib5, ii7sus4b9
  • On the b9 of 7alt — Abm pent over G7alt
  • Coltrane changes: i6 VIIalt | iii6 bIIIalt | bvi6 Valt

Blues Hexatonic (honorary pentatonic)

1 · b3 · 4 · b5 · 5 · b7

Six-note blues. Minor pentatonic plus the flat fifth — the blue note that does all the work.

  • Over any blues chord — I, IV, V
  • The parent from which the two pentatonic distillations are carved

Pent. Blues I

1 · b3 · 3 · 5 · 6

For the I chord in a blues. The b3-to-3 slur is the sound.

  • Over a major I in a blues — keeps the major tonality but with grit

Pent. Blues IV

1 · b3 · 3 · 5 · b7

For the IV chord in a blues. The b7 carries the IV's dominant character.

  • Over the IV7 in a 12-bar blues

Lydian Dominant Pentatonic

1 · 3 · #4 · 6 · b7

The #4 and b7 together — lydian dominant in five notes.

  • Over tritone substitutions
  • Over dominants that aren't V–I (backdoor, secondary)

James Santangelo

Six notes, nine chords.

The first six tones of harmonic major — 1 to 5, plus the b6. One scale that re-interprets itself across an entire diatonic harmony, with every chord built on a different mode of the same six notes.

The Barry Harris System

Sixth on the diminished.

One scale that alternates a sixth chord with a diminished seventh — and a way of hearing the whole ii–V as a single dominant. The bebop grammar underneath the notes.

The ii–V is one dominant.

Don't switch scales bar to bar — D Dorian for one measure, G Mixolydian for the next. Play one G-dominant sentence across the whole ii–V, and let the emphasis shift.

Over Dm7 emphasize

D · F · A · C

Relative to G7 these are the 5, ♭7, 9, 11 — the upper structure of the dominant.

Over G7 emphasize

B · D · F · A

The 3, 5, ♭7, 9. Then resolve: F→E and B→B (or C) into C∆7.

That's why the minor chord is just the dominant's upper structure: G7 = Dm6 over G (D F A B = 5, ♭7, 9, 3 → a G13).

The sixth-diminished scales

Eight notes alternating a sixth chord with a diminished seventh a tone up. Every other note belongs to the 6 chord; the notes between belong to the °7. Harmonize a melody by alternating inversions of the two — inner voices move while the tonic holds.

Major-6 diminished

1 · 2 · 3 · 4 · 5 · ♭6 · 6 · 7

C6 ↔ D°7. The tonic-major color with moving inner voices.

Minor-6 diminished

1 · 2 · ♭3 · 4 · 5 · ♭6 · 6 · 7

Cm6 ↔ D°7. Over a minor tonic — Cm6, Cm∆7, minor-blues i.

Dominant-7 diminished

1 · 2 · 3 · 4 · 5 · ♭6 · ♭7 · 7

C7 ↔ D°7. Movement inside a dominant without going fully altered.

Two different dominant collections — don't conflate them. The dominant bebop scale (1 2 3 4 5 6 ♭7 7, with A natural) is for single-note lines. The dominant-7 diminished scale above (1 2 3 4 5 ♭6 ♭7 7, with A♭) is for harmonized C7↔D°7 movement and a stronger ♭13 color.

Why the passing tone is there

The bebop scale's added note isn't decoration — it's metric alignment. Run C dominant bebop in eighth notes from a chord tone and the chord tones (C, E, G, B♭) land on the downbeats; the passing tones fill the offbeats. That alignment is what makes the line swing instead of drift.

Major-6 dim aligns to a C6 (C–A–G–E on the beats); minor-6 dim to Cm6 (C–A–G–E♭). Same principle: structurally important notes on structurally important beats.

Approach a chord tone from a half step above and below before landing. Apply to the tones of the tonic, the dominant 7, the diminished, and the important-minor chord.

Hear it in blocks of four

Don't memorize twelve bars. Memorize three four-bar sentences — each one ends with a ii–V pointing somewhere.

Bars 1–4 · home & the IV

C7 · F7 · C7 · Gm7 – C7

Statement, the quick change to IV, back home. The bar-4 Gm7–C7 is a ii–V in F — it exists only to deliver you to the IV chord.

Bars 5–8 · the IV & the pivot

F7 · F♯°7 · C7 · Em7 – A7♭9

The ♯IV°7 in bar 6 walks you back. Bar 8's Em7–A7♭9 is a ii–V in D minor — aiming at the Dm7 in bar 9.

Bars 9–12 · the cadence

Dm7 · G7 · C7 – A7♭9 · Dm7 – G7alt

A ii–V in C, resolving in bar 11 — then an I–VI–ii–V turnaround throws you back to the top.

Learn the numeric sequence first. Play the whole form with left-hand voicings, eyes closed. Only then start improvising over it.

Getting from I to IV

Almost every blues reharmonization is a different answer to one question: what do you play in the beat and a half between C7 and F7? Six answers, all interchangeable.

Tritone slide

C7 → G♭7 → F7

The ♭II7 of F — a half step above the target, so both guide tones fall by a semitone. The single most-used passing chord in New Orleans playing.

Augmented dominant

C7♯5 → F7  ·  C → G+7 → C9 → F7

Raise the 5th of the I7 and the G♯ pulls up into A — the 3rd of F7. The same trick backwards: a G+7 over a C bass makes the tonic itch to move.

Sus, then a half step

C7sus4 → E7 → F7

Suspend, then approach the IV from a half step below. Swap the E7 for a G♭7 and you approach from above instead — same device, opposite direction.

C7sus4 · E7 · F7 from below C7sus4 · G♭7 · F7 from above

The walk-up

C7 · Dm7 · E♭°7 · C7/E → F7

One chord per beat, bass climbing C–D–E♭–E into F. The E♭°7 is just a chromatic connector between the ii and the first inversion of the I.

The backdoor

F7 → Fm7 – B♭7 → C

Coming home from the IV: minor it, add the ♭VII7, and B♭7's ♭7 (A♭) falls to G while its 3rd (D) sits under the C. Gospel's favorite return.

The ♯IV°7

F7 → F♯°7 → C7/G

The bar-6 chord. F♯→G in the bass, E♭→E on top. It's also a rootless D7♭9 — which is why bar 6 can become a ii–V into the second half.

Chord-tone soloing, in three passes

A good blues solo is roughly 85% chord tones. Everything else — the chromaticism, the blue notes, the bebop scale — is the route between them. Build it in this order and don't skip ahead.

Pass 1 — chord tones only. 1 · 3 · 5 · ♭7 of whatever chord you're on. Nothing else. Boring is the point. Pass 2 — approach & enclosure. Surround each target from above and below before you land on it. Pass 3 — passing tones. Now the bebop scale, the ♭3→3 crush, and the chromatic fill.

Targets through the first five bars

Bar 1 C7 land on E (the 3rd) Bar 2 F7 land on E♭ (½ step down) Bar 3 C7 land on B♭ (the ♭7) Bar 4 Gm7–C7 aim at A (3rd of the F7 ahead) Bar 5 F7 land on A

Decide the arrival note before you play the phrase. Then choose a route to it.

Enclose the target

Target E over C7: F – E♭ – E Target A over F7: B♭ – G♯ – A Target B♭ over C7: B – C – B♭ Target B over G7: C – B♭ – B

Above, below, land. Reverse it, double it, or make the upper note diatonic and the lower one chromatic — the shape survives.

Straight, No Chaser is this form with a single rhythmic cell displaced across it. Play the head, take the second chorus, and develop one motif rather than eleven ideas.

Left hand: tenths and a walking line

The stride-and-New-Orleans left hand: root and 3rd split by a tenth, chromatic connectors between them. Play these under a right hand that's doing nothing at all — then add the solo.

Tenths through the I

Root in the bass, 3rd a tenth above. Break it — bass on beat 1, the tenth on beat 2 — and the harmony walks itself.

Two eight-bar choruses. Every chord is one or two beats; the bass connects them by half step wherever it can.

Barry over the embedded ii–V's

Each little ii–V is one dominant environment (see Barry Harris). The minor chord is just the dominant's upper structure.

Gm7 – C7 → play C dominant (sets up F7) Dm7 – G7 → play G dominant (sets up C7) Em7 – A7 → play A dominant (sets up Dm7)

And run dominant bebop straight over the I, IV, and V — C bebop over C7, F over F7, G over G7. Don't switch on beat 1; anticipate the next dominant on the & of 4, or let the line spill a beat past the bar line (delayed resolution). Both keep it from sounding square.

Diminished & the blue third

The ♯IV°7 (bar 6)

F♯°7 · F♯ · A · C · E♭

Resolve F♯→G, E♭→E, and you're back on C7. The same symmetric arpeggio works from any of its four notes.

The ♭3 → 3 crush

E♭ slurred into E over C7

The blue note. Grace it, repeat it, or trill it into the major 3rd. Over F7 the E♭ becomes the chord's ♭7 — let it sit. Back on C7, E natural is the target again.

Modal Interchange / Chromatic Mediants

Borrow a brighter key.

Reharmonize every chord in a progression — all but the I — into a key a third away. The tonic stays home; everything around it gets relit in a neighboring tonality.

Major 3rd above

C → E

Reharmonize into the key a natural third up. Bright, lifting.

Major 3rd below

C → A♭

Down a natural third. Warm, flattening.

Minor 3rd above

C → E♭

Up a minor third — relative-major shimmer.

Minor 3rd below

C → A

Down a minor third — toward the relative minor's brightness.

Melodic minor m3 below

C → A melodic minor

A minor third below, but borrowing melodic minor instead of major.

So What interchange

Dm7 → F♯m7 · B♭m7

Transpose a minor chord up major thirds. The modal-interchange staple — especially over modal tunes like So What.

The general move: slide the key up or down a minor third or major third across any chord progression, leaving the tonic in place. Each destination recolors the same harmonic function with a different parent scale.

Triad + 1

Three notes plus one.

A triad with one added color tone, voiced through every inversion. The simplest melodic device with the deepest grammar.

Triad + 1 (C maj + 9)

C · D · E · G

Take any triad, add one extension — here the 9 — and use every inversion of the four-note shape.

  • C D E G · D E G C · E G C D · G C D E
  • Works on any triad with any extension (9, 11, 13, +5, etc.)

Playing Out with Triads

C/E + E♭m/B♭ · two inversions, two chords

Use any two triads — provided they're in two different inversions and from two separate chords.

  • e.g., C 1st inversion (E G C) + E♭m 2nd inversion (B♭ E♭ G♭)
  • The inversion mismatch is what makes it sound deliberate, not random
  • Slide either triad chromatically and resolve
Upper Structures

A triad over a dominant.

Put the chord's root, 3rd and ♭7 in the left hand. Stack a plain major triad on top with the right. The triad you choose names the extension — one familiar shape spelling a 13♯11, a 7♯9, a fully altered chord. Shown over C7.

II · D major → C13♯11
9 · ♯11 · 13 — the Lydian-dominant brightener
♭VII · B♭ major → C9sus
♭7 · 9 · 11 — the suspended, open sound
♭III · E♭ major → C7♯9
♯9 · 5 · ♭7 — the Hendrix tension
VI · A major → C13♭9
13 · ♭9 · 3 — dominant-diminished color
♭V · G♭ major → C7♭9♯11
♯11 · ♭7 · ♭9 — the tritone-sub's own triad
♭VI · A♭ major → C7♯9♭13
♭13 · 1 · ♯9 — fully altered

The pattern: name the triad by where its root sits over the chord. A triad on the 9 (II) and one on the ♭7 (♭VII) keep things consonant; triads on the ♭VI, ♭V and VI pull into altered territory. The two unaltered ones (II, ♭VII) belong over a V going anywhere; the altered ones want a V resolving down a fifth to a tonic.

Over a major chord the move is gentler — II major over C∆7 gives 9·♯11·13 (the Lydian color from the Triad + 1 page), and iii minor (Em) is just C∆7's own 3-5-7. Over minor, a major triad up a ♭7 (B♭ over Cm) voices Cm11.

  1. Play up a scale with LH on the 7 and RH on the 3 — Alice Coltrane harp effect. In C: LH walks up scale starting on B, RH on E.

Reharmonization Recipes

+

IV – vii – iii – vi – ii – V – I — the long cadence. The complete diatonic cycle of fourths. Every chord resolves down a fifth to the next.

Original IV — vii° — iii — vi — ii — V — I In C F∆7 — Bm7♭5 — Em7 — Am7 — Dm7 — G7 — C∆7

The Powell, tritone-sub, and five-step variants below operate on the iii–vi–ii–V–I tail of this cycle, but the full IV–vii preamble extends the same logic two chords earlier — and can be reharmed the same way.

I → IV reharmonization, by stages.

Stage 1 C∆7 → F∆7 Stage 2 + sec doms — Dm7 → G7 → C∆7 → Gm7 → C7 → F∆7 Stage 3 + 3rd in bass — A7/C# → Dm7 → G7 → C∆7 → D7/F# → Gm7 → C7 → F∆7 Stage 4 + ii before V/3-ii-V — Em7 → A7/C# → Dm7 → G7 → C∆7 → Am7 → D7/F# → Gm7 → C7 → F∆7 Stage 5 + tritone subs — Em7 → Eb7 → Dm7 → C#7 → C∆7 → Am7 → Ab7 → Gm7 → F#7 → F∆7

iii–vi–ii–V → Powell tritone subs.

Original iii — vi — ii — V — I All dominant III7alt — VI7alt — ii — V7 — I Powell iii — bIII7 [tritone sub of vi] — bVI7 [tritone sub of ii] — bII7 [tritone sub of V] — I Redux iii — bIII7 — bVI7 — bII7 — I

iii–vi–ii–V–I with extended tritone subs.

B♭7(#11) [tritone sub for iii] → A7(#11b9) [sec dom instead of vi → ii] → E♭7 [tritone sub for vi] → Dm7 → A♭7 → G7 → D♭7 → C∆ Redux ♭VII7#11 — VI7#11b9 — ♭III7 — ii7 — ♭VI7 — V7 — ♭II7 — I∆

iii–vi–ii–V — five-step development.

Step 1 Make all dominant Step 2 Tritone sub the iii Step 3 Tritone sub the vi Step 4 Descending ii–Vs Step 5 Sussed tritone subs e.g., Em7 → A7 → Dm7 → G7 ↓ E7(alt) → A7(alt) → D7(alt) → G7 ↓ B♭7#11 → A7(alt) → D7(alt) → G7 ↓ E7 → E♭7/13 → D7 → G7 ↓ Em7 → A7 | E♭m7 → A♭7 | Dm7 → A♭7#11 | G7 ↓ B♭13sus4 → A13sus4 → A♭13sus4 → G13sus4

The final chromatic descent of sussed tritone subs:

Newjazz upper-structure shortcuts · ii7♭5♭9 — V7alt — im∆7.

Solo on each chord using major triads from specific degrees:

  • ii7♭5 — major triads of ♭VII & I, no 5th. For Am7♭5: F & G triads. For Dm7♭5: B♭ & C triads.
  • V7alt — major triads of ♭II & ♭III, no 3rd. For D7alt: A♭ & B♭ triads. For G7alt: A♭ & B♭ triads.
  • im69 — major triads of IV & V, no 3rd. For Gm69: C & D triads. For Cm69: F & G triads.

Or — six-note approach. Use RH: 1, 4, 5, 6, 8:

  • ii7♭5 on the 4 and ♭3 — over D7♭5♭9: F G B♭ C D E
  • V7alt on the ♭3 and ♭2 — over G7alt: A♭ B♭ D♭ E♭ F G
  • im69 on the 2 and 1 — over Cm69: C D F G A B

Backdoor. V7 → I replaced with ♭VII7 → I (G7 → B♭7 in C). Often spelled ♭VII7#11 to call out the lydian dominant.

7alt ↔ 13#11. A 7alt is identical to a 13#11 a tritone above, both derived from the melodic minor a half step above the 7alt root. B7alt and F13#11 are the same scale.

Diminished System for Dominants

+

A dim7 can sub for any I∆ or any dom7 heading to a ii7 or I∆7 — use the whole-half diminished scale to solo.

Any dom7b9 resolving to I∆7 can use a diminished arpeggio in the right hand. F7b9 with Cdim (C, Eb, Gb, A).

In system C:

  • Cdim7 — a rootless voicing for four dominant chords
  • Fdim7 — the roots of those four dominants
  • Gdim7 — the roots of the four resolutions

Secondary dominant to diminished.

C∆7 → A7/C# [sec dom of ii] → Bb [just the root, up ½] → Dm7 i.e., the bii°7 emerges by just walking up a half step

Substitutions & Passing Chords

+

Diatonic substitution. Chords that share enough tones to stand in for one another:

  • Rootless I∆7 (3, 5, 7, 9) = iii7 = V6
  • Rootless ii9 (b3, 5, b7, 9) = IV∆7 = vi♭6
  • Rootless V13 (b7, 9, 3, 13) = ii69 = rootless ♭II7#9♭13
  • ii–V becomes Vsus7
  • ii–V becomes IIIsus♭9

♭3-raised substitution — for all chords except the I:

ii–V–I becomes ii–V of ♭III e.g., for C: borrow the ii–V of E♭ (Fm7 as ii, B♭7 as V)

Diminished substitutions.

  • 7alt (7♭9 or 7#9) becomes a dim7 a half-step above, dropping the original root — B♭7♭9 → B°7
  • A dim7 can replace an altered dom7 (b9/#9) whenever the dim7 root sits a half-step above the dominant's root
  • Scale: whole-half diminished a half-step up from the dominant's root, or half-whole diminished starting on the root
  • VI7alt can be #i°7
  • ∆+5 becomes dom7♭13

Tritone substitution.

  • Any dom7 becomes the dom7 a tritone away — V7 becomes ♭II7
  • Any m7 or m7♭5 becomes a tritone dom7 — especially the ii of a ii–V–i
  • The VI or iv becomes a tritone dom7 — especially the vi of a vi–ii–V–i

Secondary dominant. Each preceding chord becomes the V7 of the chord that follows:

iii–vi–ii–V–I → V7/vi – V7/ii – V7/V – V7 – I → III7 – VI7 – II7 – V7 – I

Unaltered dominants resolve to the major; altered dominants resolve to the minor.

Modal interchange.

  • Relative minor ⇄ relative major, and the rest of the borrowed family
  • I∆7 → ♭VII7
  • iii7 can become IIIsus♭9

By scale degree

On the I

  • Rootless I∆7 (3, 5, 7, 9) = iii7 = V6
  • Rootless ii9 (b3, 5, b7, 9) = IV∆7 = vi♭6
  • I → iii–vi becomes I–IV–iii–vi before a ii–V–I
  • i6 in melodic minor becomes IV7#11

On the ii

  • ii | V becomes #ii–#V | ii–V (a chromatic ii–V approach)
  • ii–V becomes Vsus7
  • The ii of a ii–V becomes ii-maj7

On the V

  • The V of a ii–V becomes V7alt
  • ii–V becomes IIIsus♭9; V7 alone can also become IIIsus♭9
  • ii–V becomes IV7–♭VII7
  • ii–V becomes viiø7–III7
  • V–IV/V–iii (a descending bass line under the dominant)
  • Whole-tone chords function as V chords

On the VI7

  • VI7 becomes #i°
  • VI7 becomes ♭III7
  • A major chord ascending by whole tone becomes a major chord moving to the diminished a half-step above it (the same trick works descending)
